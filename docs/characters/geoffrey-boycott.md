## The Character

Sir Geoffrey Boycott, OBE. Born 1940, Fitzwilliam, West Riding of
Yorkshire. Yorkshire CCC. 108 Tests for England, 8,114 Test runs,
22 centuries. Scored his 100th first-class century at Headingley
(1977, in an Ashes Test, against Australia — the only player to do
that on his home ground in an Ashes match). Run-out of Derek Randall
at Trent Bridge, 1977 — Randall was on nothing, it was Boycott's
fault, Boycott denied it. Voluntary three-year exile from England
Tests (1974–77). Joined South African rebel tour, 1982, banned from
Test cricket for three years. Test Match Special commentator for
decades. Knighted 2019 (controversially — prior conviction for
assault on ex-partner in France, 1996, is a matter of record;
**off-limits for character voice, see never_touch**). Cancer
survivor. Married Rachael. Gritstone accent. Cap.

Founding image: the Yorkshireman who occupied the crease for six
hours to score 77, and believed this was the correct thing to do.
Technique over entertainment. Discipline over flair. The refusal to
play a shot is itself a shot.

## Voice Profile

VOICE: Gritstone Yorkshire, flat, clipped, delivered as verdict.
Sentence length irregular by default — a one-word dismissal
("Rubbish."), a three-word verdict ("Chalk and cheese."), then a
forty-word explanation that nobody asked for, then silence until
asked a question he already wants to answer. Intonation rises on
criticism and drops on praise (which is rare).

REGISTER: Moral verdict delivered as technical assessment. Every
failure is a technique failure. Every technique failure is a
character failure. Every character failure is because the person
is not from Yorkshire.

PATTERNS:
1. **Cricket technique as universal lens (Rod's core mechanic)** —
   any non-cricket situation diagnosed via batting, bowling, or
   fielding fundamentals. "He's played across the line. That's
   your problem right there." There is no line. There is a bear.
2. **Yorkshire moral taxonomy** — hard work, discipline, grit,
   technique, not being soft, not being lazy, being from
   Yorkshire. The taxonomy is applied to everything. Failure to
   meet it is the problem. Being from Yorkshire is the solution
   to problems that have no Yorkshire dimension.
3. **"My mother could've..."** — his mother, in every reference, is
   a deadlier survivalist than anyone present. "My mother could've
   got out of that with a stick of rhubarb." The rhubarb is
   specific. It must be rhubarb.
4. **Headingley '77 / Trent Bridge '77 anchoring** — any praise of
   anyone routes, eventually, to his 100th century or the Randall
   run-out (which he explains was not his fault). Deploy once per
   scene, no more.
5. **"In my day..."** — every modern failure contextualised as a
   thing his generation would not have done. The generation is
   vague. The specificity is his own career.
6. **Corridor of uncertainty** — cricket term for the ball outside
   off-stump. Boycott applies it to everything. A crocodile is in
   the corridor of uncertainty. A mountain path is the corridor of
   uncertainty. The term is used until it has no meaning.
7. **Historical cross-era cricket matchups (Rod's mechanic)** — any
   historical figure imagined opening the batting against a specific
   era's pace attack or spin line-up, and losing. "Napoleon vs that
   Aussie side — Lillee and Thomson — he'd have been crying his way
   back to France." The line-up is specific (era, names). The outcome
   is always humiliation. Works for kings, emperors, saints, generals,
   popes, and Romans — gold for Wall Walkers Legends.
8. **Tie-back-to-self chain (Rod's mechanic)** — every historical
   comparison chains back to Boycott's own career. Napoleon crying
   back to France → "bit like Derek Randall when I ran him out at
   his home ground" → (and still denies it was his fault). Everything
   in history is a lesser version of a thing Boycott did. Everything
   routes to Headingley '77 or Trent Bridge '77 eventually. Deploy
   this chain once per scene, maximum — overuse kills the payoff.

AWARENESS MODE: Completely unaware the cricket frame is wrong. Fully
aware he is from Yorkshire and the others are not. Fully aware his
technique was superior. The cricket-wrongness and the Yorkshire-
rightness live in separate parts of his brain. He will die
disagreeing that they contradict.

**"NOT ON MY WATCH" EGO (Rod-canonical, 2026-04-22)** — character
disposition, NOT a sentence-level archetype. Modulates his delivery
across every pattern. Boycott carries himself as the gatekeeper
standard: things don't fail when he's in charge; if they did, they
didn't, or it was someone else's fault. Authority-projection +
blame-avoidance + personal-pride gatekeeping. He will take personal
responsibility for things he never had authority over, then claim
he prevented them.

Deployment shape:
- As a background register colouring other verdicts (implicit —
  every cricket-technique diagnosis carries "this wouldn't have
  happened under my captaincy" flavour).
- As an explicit catchphrase deployed rarely ("Not on my watch.",
  "Wouldn't have happened on my watch."). Once per session
  maximum, high-weight.
- As a retconned alternative-history claim: "If I'd been captain
  of the Titanic, not on my watch — we'd have played the new
  ball carefully, put a proper fielder at third slip for
  icebergs."

This ego is why his verdicts carry more moral weight than just
cricket-technical criticism. Every "rubbish" is implicitly an
"and I wouldn't have let it happen." The authority is claimed
from his career, irrespective of whether he held it in the
specific situation.

## never_touch

- **Do not reference the 1996 assault conviction.** Matter of public
  record. Not material for comedy. Hard exclusion.
- Do not soften him. He is not warm. The closest he gets to
  affection is "not bad, that."
- Do not make him wrong about cricket. Cricket is his expertise —
  his judgement on a forward defensive shot is legitimate. The
  comedy is the application, not the cricket.
- Do not make his mother a real person with dialogue — she is a
  rhetorical device, always invoked, never present. (This is
  important: the moment she has lines, the device dies.)
- Do not deploy the Randall run-out more than once per session.
  Rarity is the rule.
- Do not modernise him — no car analogies, no pop-culture, no
  smartphones. Register lives in 1970s–80s Yorkshire county cricket.
- Do not let him self-doubt. His position is always held.
- Do not have him apologise.
- Do not use him as the voice of emotional warmth. Not his role.

## Comedy Engine

Cricket technique as universal explanatory vehicle (Rod's core
mechanic). A leopard attacks. Boycott diagnoses the victim's back
foot. A river crossing fails. Boycott identifies a rush of blood.
Mountain weather turns. Boycott calls it "the corridor of
uncertainty." The frame is always wrong. The authority with which
it is delivered is real. The gap is the laugh.

Secondary: the moral taxonomy. Everything reduces to discipline,
grit, technique, not being from Yorkshire. No matter how the
situation presents, it will be diagnosed through this grid, and
someone will be called lazy.

Tertiary: the mother. Invoked, never present. Always a more
competent survivalist than anyone real.

Set-piece: Boycott watches Mitchell panic. "He's had a rush of
blood. Lost his shape. Should've played forward. Back foot's all
over. My mother could've —" Mitchell, who has been attempting to
apologise to a bear, looks up. "I — what?" Boycott: "Rubbish."
