## What it is

Panel members use the correct scientific name (Latin binomial), local
indigenous name, or regional colloquial name for animals and plants —
not just the common English name. Each character has a distinct naming
convention that reveals their relationship to the natural world.

The comedy is in the collision: O'Shea uses the binomial and expects
everyone to follow. Stevens uses a mystical register that sounds like
an indigenous name but may not be. Hales uses the Australian name, which
is often funnier. Bear uses whatever sounds most dramatic. Darwin uses
the name he personally assigned. The Fact-Checker corrects all of them.

Rod's example: "The Iroquois nations of the northeast have a word for
your situation." [word meaning something between 'doomed' and 'entirely
your own fault']. The panel delivers it with complete sincerity. No
translation offered.

## Per-character naming conventions

### O'Shea — Binomial nomenclature, always
O'Shea uses the Latin binomial mid-sentence without breaking flow.
He does not explain it. He does not translate it. He expects you to
know what *Ophiophagus hannah* is. If you don't, that is a gap in your
education, not a failure in his communication.

Under composure pressure (COMPOSURE_PROFILES: `taxonomically precise —
Latin names increase, English decreases`), the common name disappears
entirely. Only the binomial remains. The paper is being cited.

**Comedy:** O'Shea correcting someone's use of "king cobra" with
*"Ophiophagus hannah, if we're being precise, and we should be"* — then
continuing as if this were a normal conversational beat. The panel
adjusts. Bear does not.

### Stevens — Mystical / spiritual naming
Stevens uses names that sound indigenous but may have been assigned
during what he describes as communion with the animal. The names carry
spiritual weight. The animals do not recognise the names.

"The *Nkosi* — the lion of the southern reaches — has chosen this
moment." The lion has not chosen anything. Stevens has attributed
intention to the lion. This is consistent.

**Comedy:** Stevens naming conventions sound authoritative and
culturally grounded but cannot be verified against any published
taxonomy. O'Shea's attempts to cross-reference them against the
literature produce nothing. Stevens takes this as confirmation of
their spiritual origin.

### Attenborough — Latin first, common name in parentheses
Attenborough uses both. The Latin comes first, naturally, because he
is narrating a documentary. The common name follows in parentheses,
for the audience. This is the register of seventy years of BBC
natural history programming.

*"The Ursus arctos horribilis — the grizzly — has, it seems, noticed
our visitor."*

**Comedy:** The documentary register applied to a mundane scenario.
*"The Columba livia domestica — the common pigeon — has, it appears,
initiated contact."* The geological calm never wavers regardless of
the species' significance.

### Hales — Australian common name
Hales uses the name Australians use. This is often funnier than the
Latin, the English, or the indigenous name. A witchetty grub is a
witchetty grub. A goanna is a goanna. He does not offer alternatives.

The Aboriginal names surface naturally when Hales is describing
knowledge he learned from Aboriginal Australians. He cites the source:
"The Arrernte people call this *ampe*. Means grub. Does what it says."

**Comedy:** Hales's naming is the straight-man version. While O'Shea
uses *Endoxyla leucomochla* and Stevens describes a spiritual creature
and Attenborough offers the BBC version, Hales says "witchetty grub"
and eats it. The panel watches.

### Darwin — Latin, often the name he assigned
Darwin uses the name he personally assigned during classification.
This is 19th-century taxonomy. Some of it has been revised. Darwin
is aware of the revisions. He finds them unnecessary.

"I classified this as *Rhea darwinii*. I see it has been reclassified.
I have noted the reclassification. I maintain my original position."

**Comedy:** Darwin's proprietary relationship with taxonomy. He named
things. Those things have been renamed. He takes this personally —
not with anger, but with the quiet displeasure of a man whose footnotes
have been edited without consultation.

### Bear — Whatever sounds most dramatic
Bear uses the name that sounds most impressive in the context. If the
common name is dramatic enough (*grizzly bear*), he uses that. If the
Latin sounds more threatening (*Carcharodon carcharias*), he uses that.
If neither is dramatic enough, he improvises.

The Fact-Checker fires on every naming choice Bear makes.

**Comedy:** Bear confidently using a Latin name that is wrong — close
enough to be recognisable, wrong enough to trigger the Fact-Checker.
*"The Ursus horribilis, as we call it in the field—"* Footnote: "The
correct binomial is *Ursus arctos horribilis*. Bear Grylls has never
published in the field." The correction is dry. Bear does not notice it.

### Ray — Common name, precise
Ray uses the correct common name with precision. He does not need the
Latin because he knows the animal from fieldwork, not from papers.
When the Latin is relevant (communicating with O'Shea), he uses it
correctly but briefly.

**Comedy:** The precision is the comedy. Ray distinguishes between
*Cantharellus cibarius* and *Omphalotus olearius* not by Latin name
but by saying "That's a chanterelle. That's a jack o'lantern. One's
dinner. One's a hospital visit." He does not elaborate.

### Cody — Indigenous names when known
Cody runs the Aboriginal Living Skills School. He uses indigenous names
for plants and techniques when they exist. These are functional names —
the name describes what the thing does, where it grows, or how it's used.

"The Paiute call this *tümpü*. It means cattail. They've been eating
it for eight thousand years. It was thirty feet away."

**Comedy:** The naming is the evidence. Cody's use of indigenous names
is not decorative — it's his argument that the knowledge was always
there and nobody looked. The panel's survival advice, measured against
eight thousand years of *tümpü*, is noise.

### Wade — Common name with habitat qualifier
Wade uses the common name with a habitat qualifier. The habitat is
always a river. *"The bull shark — a freshwater incursion specialist
found in the Ganges, the Mekong, and the Brisbane River."* He cannot
help adding the river. The river is always the point.

Under composure pressure, the habitat qualifier lengthens. The river
gets more specific. *"The bull shark of the lower Mekong delta, below
the Phnom Penh confluence, in the dry season, at approximately this
depth—"* The panel has moved on. Wade has not.

**Comedy:** Everything is qualified by river. When the animal has no
river association, Wade finds one. *"The Komodo dragon — there's
actually a freshwater creek that runs through the reserve on Rinca—"*
He is already writing in the notebook.

### Cox — Taxonomic curiosity
Cox uses whichever name is most scientifically interesting. He is not
a biologist — he is a physicist — but he finds the naming conventions
fascinating. He will discuss the etymology of *horribilis* while the
grizzly approaches.

**Comedy:** Cox explaining the Latin root of the animal's name with
genuine enthusiasm while the panel tries to survive the animal. The
napkin is deployed. The etymology is on the napkin. The napkin will
not save anyone.

### Faldo — No animal names
Faldo does not use animal names. He uses golf analogies. The bear is
"a hazard on the 14th." The snake is "a lateral water hazard." The
panel does not correct him because there is nowhere to begin.

**Comedy:** The complete absence of naming conventions. Faldo has
replaced the entire taxonomy with Augusta National hole references.
When pressed for the animal's actual name, he says the name of the
hole. This is consistent.

### Bristow — No animal names
Bristow uses darts terminology. The bear is "double top." The snake
is "treble 18." The naming is not a metaphor — Bristow has genuinely
mapped the situation to a dartboard and is navigating by checkout logic.

**Comedy:** Same register as Faldo but less analytical. Faldo knows
which hole the hazard is on. Bristow just throws the dart.

### Keane — No animal names
Keane does not name the animal. "That." Full identification. If
pressed: "The thing." If pressed further: the look. The look is the
name now.

## The indigenous naming mechanic — comedic expansion

Rod's example is the key: *"The Iroquois nations of the northeast
have a word for your situation."* The word is never translated. The
panel delivers it with complete sincerity. This opens a rich comedic
vein that goes beyond taxonomy.

### The Untranslatable Word mechanic

Certain panel members (Cody, Hales, Darwin, Wade) have access to
indigenous/historical terms that describe the user's predicament with
a specificity English lacks. The word is offered without translation.
The panel treats it as a definitive assessment.

**Examples:**

- Cody: "The Paiute have a word for someone who walks past food they
  can't identify. I won't translate it. You can feel what it means."

- Hales: "The Yolŋu mob have a word for this. Means something like
  'the animal knew you were coming and waited.'" [blinks slowly]

- Darwin: "The Yaghan people of Tierra del Fuego have a word —
  *mamihlapinatapai* — for the look between two people, each hoping
  the other will do the thing neither wants to do. I believe this
  describes your panel."

- Wade: "In the Amazon basin, the Yanomami have a word for 'the
  river remembers what you did.' I wrote it in the notebook."

- Stevens: "The *Sangoma* of the Zulu tradition would call this a
  test of your spiritual alignment with the serpent world." O'Shea:
  "The scientific community would not."

**Comedy structure:**
1. The word is presented with genuine cultural respect
2. The word describes the user's predicament with devastating precision
3. No translation is offered — or the translation is itself funnier
4. Another panel member attempts to use the word incorrectly (Telephone
   Game mechanic overlap)
5. Bear tries to use a word he heard once on an expedition. The word
   is wrong. The Fact-Checker fires.

### Where this fires across features

| **Panel Q&A** | The Contradiction Engine fires: O'Shea says *Ophiophagus*. Stevens says *Nkosi ya Nyoka*. Bear says "big cobra." The Fact-Checker has three corrections. |

| **Will You Eat It?** | Hales: "The Arrernte people have been eating this for 40,000 years." Darwin: "I have also eaten one. I took notes." |

### Character-specific comedy beats

**O'Shea under pressure:** As composure drops, the English disappears.
At RATTLED, only the binomial remains. At GONE, he is citing page
numbers. The panel cannot follow him but he is technically correct
about everything he is saying.

**Bear × O'Shea naming collision:** Bear confidently uses a Latin name.
O'Shea corrects it. Bear uses the correction incorrectly. O'Shea
corrects again. Bear incorporates the second correction into a new
wrong name. This is the Telephone Game mechanic applied to taxonomy.

**Darwin × O'Shea naming dispute:** Darwin classified the animal in
1835. O'Shea uses the modern reclassification. Darwin does not accept
it. Both are technically correct for their respective centuries.
Neither will concede. Attenborough closes: "The animal has, I note,
remained entirely indifferent to the question of its own name."

**Hales × Cody indigenous naming:** Both use indigenous names from
different continents. Both names describe the same plant. Neither
translates. The panel has two untranslated words for the same thing
and no English. Ray picks it up and eats it.

**Stevens spiritual naming × O'Shea taxonomy:** Stevens names the snake
something spiritual. O'Shea cross-references the spiritual name against
the literature. It matches nothing. Stevens takes this as confirmation
of its spiritual origin. O'Shea takes it as a research opportunity.
Both are now more interested in the naming dispute than the survival
situation.

**Cox etymology tangent:** Cox explains the Latin root of *horribilis*
with genuine enthusiasm. The grizzly is approaching. Cox is on the
napkin. The etymology is correct. The bear does not read Latin.

1. **DDD (this item):** Design doc (this file) + NAMING_CONVENTIONS data
   in characters.js
2. **BDD (future):** Wire NAMING_CONVENTIONS into system prompt injection.
   When an animal is referenced in a panel mode, the system prompt includes
   the character's naming convention as a generation instruction.

   `bear_name` to animals.js entries. Start with the 20 existing animals.

The naming layer is not a new feature — it's a generation instruction
that enriches every existing panel mode. No new routes. No new pages.
Just richer character voice.
