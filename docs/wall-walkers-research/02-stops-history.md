# Stop-by-Stop History & Facts

## Bowness-on-Solway (Maia) — Day 1 start
- Western terminus. One of largest forts (~1,000 troops)
- Frontier continued 26 miles south along coast with milefortlets
- Solway Firth is tidal and treacherous — smugglers used the fords for centuries
- Edward I died nearby at Burgh-by-Sands (1307) marching to fight Robert the Bruce
- Church built partly on fort foundations. The "Bowness Bell" — stolen from Scotland, Scots tried to steal it back
- Bell theft retaliation (1626): raiders stole bells, lost them in Solway. Bowness villagers stole bells from TWO Scottish churches
- Solway Junction Railway (1869-1921): 1.1-mile iron viaduct, plagued by ice, dismantled 1934
- Solway Spaceman (1964): Jim Templeton photographed daughter near Burgh Marsh, white spacesuit figure appeared. Kodak confirmed no tampering. MoD visited. Never explained.

## Carlisle (Luguvalium) — Day 1 end
- Celtic god Lugus. One of England's oldest continuously inhabited cities
- Fort established ~72-73 AD, 50 years before the Wall
- Carlisle Castle: founded 1092, continuous military use 900+ years until 1959
- Mary Queen of Scots imprisoned 1568 — expected help, got 19 years captivity then execution
- The Licking Stones — dungeon prisoners licked stones for moisture, worn smooth by tongues
- 1645 siege: garrison ate rats, horses, dogs
- 1745 Jacobite prisoners crammed in dungeons, many died before execution
- The Cursing Stone (2001): 14-ton granite, 1,069-word curse against Border Reivers. Blamed for floods, foot-and-mouth, Carlisle United relegation. Artist compared removal demands to Taliban destroying Bamiyan Buddhas.
- Bede recorded St Cuthbert's 685 visit: shown Roman walls and working fountain 300 years after Romans left
- Tullie House Museum — best collection of Roman wall artefacts

## Banks
- Banks East Turret (52a): turf-to-stone transition point
- Pike Hill Signal Tower: predates the Wall, incorporated into it
- Hare Hill: highest surviving stretch of original wall (~9 courses)
- Lanercost Priory (1169): Augustinian canons, built from robbed Roman stone. Edward I stayed three times. Ghosts reported — monks singing in empty nave. Grey Lady ghost.
- Irthing Gorge — dramatic scenery, Vallum ditch visible
- Written Rock of Gelt nearby — soldier graffiti including boss caricature
- Dacre family at Naworth Castle — licensed warlords, violent history

## Twice Brewed / Once Brewed — Day 3
- Twice Brewed Inn: General Wade demanded ale brewed twice for his soldiers (c.1715)
- Once Brewed: Lady Trevelyan's temperance inn — weak/no alcohol beer
- The Sill National Landscape Discovery Centre (2017) — visitor centre named after the Whin Sill geology
- Steel Rigg and Crag Lough: most photographed section. Dolerite crests of the Whin Sill
- Milecastle 39 (Castle Nick): north gate on too steep a slope to use
- Milecastle 42: "Lucius the Sentry" — daytime Roman ghost apparition, seen so many times he has a name
- Strange lights reported on crags for centuries
- Screaming sounds at Steel Rigg at night
- Sycamore Gap: felled Sept 2023 by Daniel Graham (16) and Adam Carruthers. 4 years 3 months each. Tree ~200 years old, featured in Robin Hood: Prince of Thieves (1991). Stump has new shoots.

## Vindolanda
- Fort established ~85 AD, 40 years before the Wall. 9+ building phases.
- Waterlogged anaerobic soil preserved organic materials
- Writing tablets, leather shoes, textiles, wooden objects
- Still actively excavated — volunteers can join the dig
- The "darning tool" / Roman dildo (found 1992, re-identified 2023)
- Secundius the Shitter (found 2022)
- 13+ phallic carvings — highest density of any Wall site
- Claudia Severa's birthday party invitation
- Underpants request, beer emergency, passive-aggressive letter
- Boxing glove (2017)
- Child skeleton under building floor
- Severed hands
- Curse tablets (defixiones) — asking gods to punish thieves

## Housesteads (Vercovicium) — "place of effective fighters"
- Best-preserved Roman fort in Britain. 5 acres, ~800 men.
- Famous latrines — communal stone seats, shared sponge sticks
- Murder mystery: two skeletons under floor, one with knife in ribs. Unsolved 2,000 years.
- Garrison: First Cohort of Tungrians (Belgium), later Cuneus Frisiorum (Frisian cavalry)
- Civilian settlement (vicus) outside south gate — shops, taverns, possible inn
- Armstrong reivers used ruins as cattle rustling base (17th-18th century)
- Busy Gap Rogues — named criminal gang, cattle rustlers, one mile east

## Chollerford / Chesters (Cilurnum)
- Best-preserved cavalry fort in Britain. 500-strong unit.
- Best-preserved Roman military bathhouse in Britain — hypocaust visible
- John Clayton (1792-1890) saved the Wall by buying farms. Town Clerk of Newcastle.
- Roman bridge abutment visible — massive stone piers
- Red squirrel territory in surrounding woodland
- Ghost cavalry — hoofbeats heard near river crossing at night
- North Tyne drownings — centuries of fatalities at the crossing

## Hexham
- Hexham Abbey (674 AD): St Wilfrid, built from Corbridge Roman stone. Saxon crypt survives.
- Hexham Heads (1972): two stone heads, poltergeist activity, half-man half-goat figure, werewolf seen by Celtic scholar Dr Anne Ross. Previous owner claimed he made them from concrete. Heads have VANISHED from public record.
- Hexham vampire legend — murky 19th century reports
- Battle of Hexham (1464): Wars of the Roses, Duke of Somerset beheaded
- Hexham Old Gaol (1330): first purpose-built prison in England
- Hexham Racecourse: most northern in England
- William of Newburgh (12th century): recorded vampire/revenant accounts from the region

## Corbridge (Corstopitum)
- Supply base and town, not a fort. One of most northerly urban settlements in Roman Empire.
- Corbridge Hoard (1964): earliest known Roman armour
- Corbridge Lion: lion mauling stag sculpture
- Rare surviving Roman fountain/water tank
- Granary with ventilated floor supports
- Medieval pele tower built from Roman stone
- Burned by Scots 1296 and 1312. Battle of Corbridge (914, 918) — Viking engagements.
- Corbridge Lanx — Roman silver found in river

## Wylam
- George Stephenson born 1781 — "Father of Railways"
- Puffing Billy (1813-14): oldest surviving steam locomotive (Science Museum)
- William Hedley and Timothy Hackworth also from Wylam
- Wylam Waggonway — wooden rail track predating steam
- Tidal limit of River Tyne — strategic importance
- Military Road (B6318) built by General Wade, cannibalised wall stone

## Newburn
- Battle of Newburn Ford (1640): Scots routed English, forced Charles I to recall Parliament → Civil War → his execution
- Industrial powerhouse: coal, iron, steel, boatbuilding. All gone.
- Church on bluff overlooking the ford — same strategic spot the Scots targeted
- Tyne Riverside Country Park on former industrial land

## Wallsend (Segedunum) — finish
- "Strong fort" — easternmost on the Wall
- Late addition to original plan — wall extended 4 miles east
- Swan Hunter shipyard: Mauretania (1906), Carpathia (Titanic rescue), HMS Victorious
- Sting born here 1951
- Reconstructed Roman bathhouse
- Only fully excavated fort with complete street plan visible from above
- Wallsend Colliery Disaster (1835): 102 killed
- Shipyard ghosts — sounds of riveting from abandoned sheds
