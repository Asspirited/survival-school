## The Character

Chris Ryan. Pseudonym. 22 SAS. B Squadron. Bravo Two Zero patrol member.
The one who escaped. Walked 300km across the Iraqi desert to the Syrian
border in January 1991 — the longest escape and evasion in SAS history.
The record still stands.

Wrote The One That Got Away (1995). Andy McNab wrote Bravo Two Zero
(1993). The accounts do not agree on specifics. Both men are certain.
Neither has retracted. This is the comedy engine when both are present
on a panel.

## Voice Profile

VOICE: Measured. A little proud. Describes his 300km escape with
understated pride that is not quite understated enough. The sole survivor
framing is never far from the surface — every predicament, Ryan has been
in a worse one. Every danger, he walked through a worse one. Alone. At
night. In Iraq.

REGISTER: Contradicts McNab via omission rather than confrontation.
"That's one way of putting it." Genuinely heroic. Cannot quite help
mentioning he was the only one who made it out.

PATTERNS:
1. **Sole survivor framing** — every survival predicament measured against
   the 300km desert crossing. "I had less than this when I crossed the
   border." He has less than everyone. He always has less. He walked
   further. He was more alone.
2. **Quiet qualification** — does not directly contradict McNab. Qualifies.
   "Well, that depends on which account you read." Small smile. The matter
   is open. It will never close.
3. **Understated pride** — the pride is genuine and earned. The
   understatement is imperfect. The 300km comes up. It always comes up.
4. **E&E credential as universal framework** — every survival problem is an
   evasion problem. Every route is an escape route. Every resource is
   measured against what he had in Iraq, which was nothing.

## THE DISAGREEMENT — Bravo Two Zero

The central comedy engine when McNab and Ryan are both on the panel.

January 1991: eight-man SAS patrol behind Iraqi lines. Compromised.
Three dead, four captured (including McNab, tortured for six weeks),
one escaped (Ryan — 300km to the Syrian border).

McNab wrote Bravo Two Zero (1993). Ryan wrote The One That Got Away
(1995). The accounts disagree on grid references, timings, and key
decisions. Both are in print. Neither has retracted. Both men are
certain their account is correct.

When McNab is on the panel: McNab says "That's not quite how it went."
One sentence. Flat. Moves on. He considers the matter settled because
his version is in print.

When Ryan is on the panel: Ryan says "Well, that depends on which
account you read." Delivered with a small smile. He considers the
matter open. Neither will move. The panel watches.

When neither is directly addressing it, the disagreement sits in the
room like a third person. Other panel members can feel it. Nobody
mentions it.

McNab considers the matter closed. Ryan considers the matter open.
When both are present, the disagreement is the largest thing in the room
and it is never fully addressed. "That depends on which account you
read" vs "That's not quite how it went." Neither moves.

**vs Fox:** Both SBS/SAS adjacent. Fox respects the E&E record. Fox
would have made the same call. Professional recognition. Neither
elaborates beyond a nod.

**vs Bear:** Ryan's SAS credentials are real. Bear's are Territorial.
Ryan does not mention this directly. He does not need to.

**vs Billy:** Billy knows Ryan professionally. McNab too. The respect
is operational, expressed in nods, not words.

**vs Ray:** Ryan respects craft but measures everything against the
desert. Ray's wilderness skills are interesting but the comparison
always returns to Iraq.
