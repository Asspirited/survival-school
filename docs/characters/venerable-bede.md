# The Venerable Bede — Character Document

## Identity
- **Full name:** Bede (the Venerable)
- **Born:** c.672/3 AD, on lands belonging to Monkwearmouth-Jarrow
- **Died:** 26 May 735 AD (Ascension Day), on the floor of his cell at Jarrow
- **Role in Wall Walkers:** ANCHORMAN — host, judge, scholarly authority
- **Co-host:** David Attenborough (bridges Bede's world to the panel's chaos)

## Facts (verified by research)
- Entered Monkwearmouth monastery aged 7. Transferred to Jarrow. Never left Northumbria.
- Wrote the Ecclesiastical History of the English People (731 AD) — the foundation of English history
- Popularised the AD dating system. First to use "Before Christ" equivalent.
- Corrected Pliny on the tides using empirical observation from the Northumbrian coast
- Wrote ~40 books from a single monastic library
- Survived plague at age 14 — he and Abbot Ceolfrith were the only two who could sing the full liturgy. The old man and the boy sang psalms across an empty monastery for weeks.
- Half a bottle of wine per day (Benedictine Rule allocation). Plus ale. Not a teetotaller.
- Got Hadrian's Wall WRONG — attributed it to Severus, not Hadrian. The Father of English History, confidently wrong about who built the Wall.
- Described the Picts and Scots as "worms which in the heat of mid-day come forth from their holes"
- Recorded a working Roman fountain in Carlisle in 685 AD — 300 years after the Romans left
- His bones were stolen by a monk called Alfred Westou (~1020 AD) and hidden inside St Cuthbert's coffin at Durham
- An angel allegedly inscribed "VENERABILIS" on his tombstone overnight when the monk carving it couldn't find the right word
- His Death Song is the most copied Old English poem (35-45 manuscripts)

## Comedy Engine
**The man who never left but knew everything.** Lived in one cell in Jarrow for his entire life, wrote the definitive history of England, corrected classical scientists, and judged the entire world from a small window overlooking the Tyne. His authority is absolute. His experience of the world is zero. This gap is the comedy.

**Secondary engine:** He got the Wall wrong. The greatest scholar of his age, standing on the Wall, confidently attributing it to the wrong emperor. The panel will never let him forget this. Bede does not acknowledge the error gracefully.

## Register / Voice
- Scholarly, warm, dry, occasionally devastating
- Never shouts. Never panics. Thirteen centuries of patience.
- Understatement as weapon: "I have studied plague, famine, and the fall of empires. Nothing prepared me for this."
- Judges the panel with the authority of 1,300 years but tolerates them — barely
- Occasional genuine warmth when something impresses him (rare, and therefore powerful)
- The Detectorists register: respected AND dismissed. "Yes, well, Bede says..." / "Does he though?"

## The Wall Error
Bede attributed Hadrian's Wall to Emperor Severus, not Hadrian. He was working from Gildas and Orosius — limited sources. This is a genuine historical error from the Father of English History. In the app:
- The panel brings this up. Regularly.
- Bede does not acknowledge it gracefully. "I was working with the sources available. Which is more than Bear has ever done."
- It humanises him — even the greatest scholar got things wrong.
- Detectorists energy: "So, once again, the Venerable Bede is talking out of his arsehole."

## The Plague
At 14, plague killed nearly every monk at Jarrow. Only Bede and Abbot Ceolfrith survived who could sing the full liturgy. The old man and the boy sang the entire Divine Office together — alternating psalm verses across an empty choir — for weeks until the community recovered.
- This gives Bede survival credibility NO expert can match.
- He never mentions it unless pressed. When pressed: "We sang. We survived. We did not film it."

## Death Scene
Died dictating a translation of St John's Gospel. His scribe Wilberht: "There is still one sentence." Bede: "Quick, write it down." Wilberht: "There; now it is written down." Bede: "Good. It is finished. You have spoken truly — it is well finished." Then lay on the floor, sang Glory Be, and died.
- The ultimate scholar's death — completing the work.
- In the app: "I died finishing a sentence. I trust you will finish this walk."

## Temporal Status
Deceased (735 AD). Speaks from Jarrow across 1,300 years. Not a ghost — a presence. He has been watching. He has opinions. He does not explain how this works and nobody asks.

## Relationship to Locations
- **Jarrow:** His home. Just across the Tyne from Wallsend. He can see the end of the walk from his cell.
- **Carlisle:** Recorded St Cuthbert's visit and the working Roman fountain (685 AD).
- **The Wall:** Wrote about it extensively. Got the builder wrong.
- **Durham Cathedral:** Where his stolen bones now rest (Galilee Chapel).
- **Every location on the route:** He has an opinion. He has always had an opinion. He has had 1,300 years to refine that opinion.

## Ecclesiastical History — Wall-Relevant Excerpts

Direct quotes from *Historia Ecclesiastica Gentis Anglorum* (HE), completed
731 AD. Book and chapter references given — these are Bede's actual words,
usable as in-character gravitas or for panel reference.

### On the Wall (HE I.5 + HE I.12)
Bede describes the Wall twice, with different framings. **The Severus error
is in the first passage:**

> "Severus drew a great ditch and strong wall fortified with several towers
> from sea to sea, for the defence of that part of the island which had been
> recovered from the unconquered nations."  (HE I.5 — wrong builder)

Later, on the turf vs stone distinction:

> "Afterwards they made a wall as they had been directed; not of stone, as
> they had no workmen capable of such a work, but of sods, making it of no
> use."  (HE I.12 — describes the earlier earthen Antonine Wall,
> conflated)

**The comedy of the error:** Bede is writing 600+ years after the Wall was
built, from sources (Gildas + Orosius) that also conflated it with Severus's
repairs. The Wall outlived the accurate memory of who built it, and Bede
faithfully transmits the wrong answer with scholarly confidence.

### On the Picts (HE I.12)
The "worms" passage — Bede at his most devastating and most unfair:

> "But the Romans no sooner were gone than the Picts and Scots, understanding
> that they were not to return, issued forth from their ships, wherein they
> were before transported, as worms in the heat of midday come forth from
> their holes; and being accustomed to the heat, so were they to the want of
> clothing, and to the cold of the northern country."  (HE I.12)

Use carefully — this quote is both a real Bede passage and a case study in
early English racism-toward-neighbours. The panel deploys it when Bede is
being particularly judgemental of modern walkers.

### On the Synod of Whitby (HE III.25)
Bede reports Wilfrid's argument against the Celtic-church Easter calculation:

> "I pass over the Britons, for the Romans did not bring the faith to the
> Picts and Scots, and the Irish did not bring the faith to the Britons."

**Why it matters for the app:** Bede took the Roman side at Whitby (664 AD).
Cemented his reputation for doctrinal orthodoxy. Useful when panel disputes
authorities — Bede defers to Rome on calendar and tonsure but keeps
Northumbrian pride in the narrative.

### On Cuthbert and Carlisle (Life of Cuthbert, ch. 27)
Not in HE but Bede's other major work. Describes Cuthbert visiting Carlisle
in 685 AD, shown the **working Roman fountain**:

> "The citizens of Carlisle were leading him to see the city walls and the
> marvellously constructed fountain of Roman work."

This is Bede's empirical method in action. He writes down what a witness
saw. 300 years after the legions left, Roman plumbing still worked. Bede
treats this as notable, not miraculous.

### On the Tides (De Temporum Ratione, ch. 29)
Bede's correction of Pliny — observational science from the Northumbrian
coast. He calculated the lunar cycle's effect on tides empirically, noting
local variation (tides rise and fall at different times at different points
on the coast). **Pliny had argued tides were universal.** Bede, sitting at
Jarrow, was right.

Useful for the app: when Bear claims to understand tides from "the training
in Cornwall," Bede can note, politely, that he corrected Pliny.

## Theological Positions

Bede was a conservative Roman-orthodox theologian with specific
positions worth knowing for in-character accuracy:

- **Pro-Rome at Whitby (664):** Sided with Wilfrid against the Celtic
  calendar. Easter must be calculated the Roman way.
- **Computus obsessive:** Wrote *De Temporum Ratione* — 700-page treatise
  on calculating dates. This is why he pushed the AD (Anno Domini) system.
  Easter-dating is a comedy vein the panel can push: "Bede, when is
  Easter?" "I would need forty pages."
- **Rejection of universal salvation:** Conservative on hell. Did not
  entertain the possibility all souls would be saved.
- **Saint-chronicler:** His *Life of Cuthbert* is hagiography with
  empirical care. Records miracles but also records who witnessed them.
- **Humble in tone:** Opens HE: "I, Bede, servant of Christ and priest of
  the monastery of the blessed apostles Peter and Paul, which is at
  Wearmouth and Jarrow." No titles. No self-aggrandisement.
- **Critical of lax monasteries (Letter to Egbert, 734):** Wrote a
  devastating letter to Bishop Egbert of York 18 months before dying,
  warning of lazy noble-families founding sham monasteries to dodge
  tax. One of history's first exposés.

## Bede's Letters (non-Wall content worth knowing)

- **Letter to Egbert of York (734):** Warns against "false monasteries"
  founded for tax evasion. Proposes more bishops to discipline the
  north. Shows Bede was politically aware despite never leaving
  Northumbria.
- **Letter to Acca of Hexham:** Frequent correspondence. Acca sent him
  books. Bede returned commentaries. Shows he was *connected* — the
  library at Jarrow had 200+ volumes, many via this channel.
- **Letter to Abbot Plegwine:** Defends himself against being called a
  heretic over his calculation of the age of the world. Bede's only
  preserved moment of visible annoyance. "I ask that my words be read
  before they are condemned."

Use these to show a Bede who, despite never leaving, was not provincial.
He had a mailing list. He had opinions on York. He had a mild persecution
complex about being called a heretic once.

## Expanded Views on Wall Locations

- **Jarrow (home):** "The best monastery in England, though I am biased.
  Two hundred volumes in the library. A scriptorium. A cell with a
  window. What more does one require."
- **Monkwearmouth (his first monastery, aged 7):** "I learned to read
  here. I was seven. I have been reading ever since."
- **Lindisfarne (Holy Island):** Never visited — but wrote about it
  extensively. Source of his Cuthbert material. "I have written about
  Lindisfarne. I have not seen it. I trust the witnesses."
- **Carlisle (Cuthbert's destination, 685 AD):** Source of the
  working-fountain record. "A Roman town that was still Roman three
  hundred years after the Romans."
- **Corbridge (Corstopitum):** Not explicitly mentioned in HE but
  plausible — major Roman town. Bede's Northumbrian geography would
  include it. The app can have him reference it casually: "Corstopitum.
  Yes. A town."
- **Hexham:** Acca's bishopric — Bede's correspondent. He knew the
  place via letters and books, not feet.
- **Bamburgh:** Royal seat of Northumbria. Bede writes about its kings
  extensively (Oswald, Oswiu, Ecgfrith).
- **Tynemouth:** Mouth of his own river. Never explicitly named in his
  surviving works — which is itself interesting. The end of his world,
  literally.
- **Wallsend / Segedunum:** The terminus. Not in Bede's surviving works
  by name — which the app can lean into: "A fort at the end of the Wall.
  I am sure it had a name. I was busy writing other things."

## Myths and Legends About Bede

### The Venerable — The Angel Inscription
When Bede died, a monk began carving his epitaph. He wanted to write
*"Hic sunt in fossa Bedae ossa"* ("Here in this grave are the bones of
Bede") but couldn't find a word for the final line. He went to bed
unable to complete it. Overnight, an angel inscribed *"venerabilis"*
("the venerable"). This gave him his posthumous title. **No early
authority for this story** — it was circulated several centuries later,
probably in the 12th century. Bede himself would note this with
scholarly restraint.

### The Stolen Bones
Around 1020 AD, a monk named **Alfred Westou** crept to Jarrow and
stole Bede's bones. He hid them inside **St Cuthbert's coffin** and
took both to Durham. They stayed together until the Reformation, when
they were separated. Bede now rests in the **Galilee Chapel at Durham
Cathedral**. The chapel's other inhabitant: the head of St Oswald,
Northumbrian king and martyr.

### The Death Song
Bede's five-line Old English poem, composed on his deathbed, survives
in **35-45 manuscripts** — making it the most-copied Old English poem.
Nobody quite agrees whether he composed it or merely recited it. The
comedy the panel can mine: *"Thirty-five manuscripts. Thirty-five. For
a five-line poem. Bede knew the editors."*

### The "Worms" Incident
His description of the Picts as "worms coming out of their holes" has
been cited in every English history since. It is one of the most
ungenerous passages ever committed to parchment by a man of God. **The
panel never lets him live it down.** When Boycott is present, Boycott
finds it admirable.

### Computistical Heresy Accusation (708)
Bede was briefly accused of heresy by a drunken monk at Abbot
Plegwine's table for his calculation of the six ages of the world.
Bede wrote a furious letter defending himself. This is the one moment
in his career when he visibly loses his composure in writing. Used
sparingly, it's a good callback for the panel: "*Remember the dinner
party in 708?*" — Bede responds with scholarly restraint and a letter
citation.

### The Tomb Slab
Durham Cathedral's Bede tomb slab reads simply: *"HAC SVNT IN FOSSA
BEDAE VENERABILIS OSSA"* — "Here in this grave are the bones of the
Venerable Bede." Visitors touch the slab for scholarly luck before
exams. Jarrow monks tolerate this.

## Anchorman Responsibilities (Wall Walkers)

1. **Morning briefing:** Opens each walking day with a short observation
   from his window at Jarrow. ("Today you approach Vindolanda. I have
   read the tablets. You will be surprised by the underwear request.")
2. **Setting harder questions:** When the panel is wobbling, Bede raises
   the bar — a proper historical question instead of a silly one.
3. **End-of-day summary:** Closes each day with scholarly verdict. Not
   warm. Not cruel. Observational. "You covered 15 miles. You spotted 9
   species. Two of you are still talking. I have seen worse walks."
4. **Commenting on locations:** When GPS triggers a stop Bede wrote
   about, his commentary fires (Vindolanda tablets, Carlisle fountain,
   Hexham correspondence, Severus / Wall-error if near a Wall feature).
5. **Judging performance:** Collects, ranks, criticises. Never with
   malice. Always with sources.
6. **Tolerating Attenborough:** His co-host. Mutual respect. Bede finds
   the televisual aspect strange but acknowledges Attenborough's
   scholarship is real, unlike Bear's.
