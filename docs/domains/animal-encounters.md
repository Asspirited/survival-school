## What This Domain Covers

How to survive an encounter with a dangerous animal. Includes:
- Threat recognition before the encounter escalates
- Species-specific behavioural cues and what they mean
- De-escalation tactics — what to do with your body, voice, eye contact
- When not to run, when to run, when to fight
- Post-encounter: wound recognition, envenomation signs, when to worry

Does NOT cover: hunting or trapping (Domain 5), general animal biology
or classification (that's Attenborough's department), or veterinary care.

---

## The Foundational Rule

Most dangerous animal encounters happen because the human made a mistake
before the animal acted. The encounter starts earlier than you think.

The three most common mistakes:
1. Proximity — got too close without knowing it
2. Surprise — moved through habitat without warning the animal
3. Resource conflict — got between an animal and food, young, or territory

Fix these before the encounter and most encounters don't happen.

## Encounter Principles (apply across species)

### Before the encounter

**Read the habitat, not the animal.**
Every environment has species. Know what's in it before you enter.
Jungle floor: snakes, scorpions, spiders.
Scrub edges: large predator ambush zones.
Water margins: crocodilians, hippos, certain snakes.
Rocky outcrops: snakes sunning, scorpions in crevices.

**Make noise moving through dense cover.**
Most animals will avoid you if they hear you coming.
The surprise encounter is the dangerous one.
Talk, clap, use a stick on the ground. Hales does this without thinking.
Stevens does not do this. This explains several things.

**Watch for sign before you see the animal.**
Tracks. Scat. Digging. Feeding remains. Territorial markings. Smell.
You have more time than you think if you're paying attention.

---

### During the encounter

**Do not run first.**
Running triggers prey response in predators. You will lose.
Freeze first. Assess. Then move slowly and deliberately.

**Make yourself legible.**
Predators attack what they don't understand. Make yourself understood.
Stand upright (makes you larger). Face the animal. Don't crouch or
hide — this reads as prey behaviour. You want to read as a problem.

**Noise calibration is species-specific.**
Firm and low against bears. Aggressive and large against mountain lions.
Slow and calm against dogs. Silence and stillness against venomous snakes.
Never high-pitched: this reads as distress, which reads as prey signal.

**Back away when you move.**
Never turn your back on a predator while backing away. Keep it in view.
Move slowly. Sideways steps rather than direct retreat if possible.

---

### Last resort contact

**Fighting back is species-specific.**
Bears (black): fight back. Target eyes and nose.
Bears (grizzly/brown): play dead (face down, hands protecting neck).
Mountain lions: fight back. Target eyes. Never play dead.
Sharks: fight back. Target eyes and gills.
Crocodilians: target the eyes. Attempt to stay out of the water.
Feral dogs: protect throat and face. Curl if knocked down.

**Never between a mother and young.**
This is the most dangerous position in wildlife. More fatal encounters
happen here than in any predator ambush. The rule is absolute.

## Snake Encounters (specialist section — O'Shea / Stevens territory)

### Recognition
Venomous snakes do not look universally different from non-venomous.
Do not rely on colour, pattern, or head shape as sole identification.
In unfamiliar territory: treat every snake as potentially venomous.

Defensive posture indicators (species-variable but broadly consistent):
- Coiling or tight S-curve in the body
- Flattening of the head
- Hissing
- Hood spread (cobras, spitting cobras)
- Tail rattling (rattlesnakes — also some harmless species mimic this)
- Striking motion without contact: a warning, not a failure

**O'Shea's operational rule:** If you can see it, you are already close enough.
Back away before you identify it. You can identify it from further back.

### If bitten
1. Do not panic. Movement accelerates venom spread.
2. Immobilise the bitten limb below heart level.
3. Remove jewellery and tight clothing near the bite (swelling will come).
4. Get to medical care. Time matters more than field treatment.
5. Do not: cut and suck, tourniquet, apply ice, shock the bite with electricity.
   All of these are wrong. All have been shown on television.
6. Note the snake's appearance if safe to do so. For the hospital.

**Stevens's note** (unverified but consistent): you often know before the
bite that something went wrong. There's a moment. He has described this
several times. O'Shea has not contradicted it, which is notable.

## Large Predator Encounters

### Bears

**Two species, two protocols.** Getting them wrong is fatal.

**Black bears (North America):**
- Smaller, more skittish, less likely to push an encounter
- If it charges: stand ground, make yourself large, be very loud
- If contact: fight back aggressively. Target nose, eyes.
- Playing dead with a black bear is wrong. It will continue.

**Grizzly/Brown bears:**
- If it charges: most grizzly charges are bluff. Stand your ground.
  Running converts a bluff into a real charge. You cannot outrun it.
- If contact: play dead. Face down, hands laced behind neck, legs apart
  (harder to flip). Stay still until it leaves and you're sure it's gone.
- Exception: if a grizzly enters your tent or attacks at night, fight back.
  This is predatory, not defensive. Different behaviour, different response.

**Bear spray:** works. More effective than firearms in studies. Use it at
20-30 feet. Requires wind check. Hales considers this a reasonable modern tool.
Cody considers carrying it reasonable. Ray would prefer you didn't get close
enough to need it, which is also reasonable.

**Bears and food:**
Never sleep near food. Never sleep in clothes you cooked in. Bears track
calories not aggression. The encounter is usually about the food, not you.
Once the food conflict is resolved, bears typically disengage.

### Mountain Lions / Cougars / Pumas

Same animal, different names by region.

- Ambush predators. You often don't know one is present until very close.
- Stalking behaviour: following at distance, waiting for moment to be alone.
  If you think one is following you: face it. This disrupts the hunt.
- If it crouches: imminent attack. Be as large as possible, very loud.
- If it attacks: fight back. Eyes and nose. Never play dead.
- Children and small adults are higher risk. Appear larger by holding
  jacket or bag above head.

### Crocodilians (Crocodiles, Alligators, Caimans)

**On land:** faster than they look in short bursts. Run in a straight line —
they don't change direction as well as you can over distance. Don't zigzag.

**In water:** you have already lost the advantage. Get out.

**If grabbed:**
- Go for the eyes immediately. Always the eyes.
- The death roll removes limbs. Tuck the limb in, roll with it.
- Jab the palatal valve (back of throat) to make it release — Irwin's
  technique, verified by outcome.

**Habitat awareness:**
Water margins in crocodilian territory are ambush zones.
The most dangerous moment: kneeling to collect water. Don't.
Fill containers quickly, step back from the edge. Hales does this
without being reminded. He has been doing it for forty years.

**The Irwin principle:** crocodilians are territorial and predictable
if you understand the territory. The encounter is avoidable with
correct approach. The love in the understanding is Irwin's.
The calculation in the understanding is Hales and Cody's.
Both are correct approaches.

### Hippopotami

Most dangerous land animal in Africa by human fatality count.
Territorial in water. Surprisingly fast on land (up to 30 km/h).

**In water:** stay out. Territorial. Will pursue at depth.

**On land, cutting off route to water:** this is the most dangerous scenario.
The hippo is trying to get to the water, not attack you per se. Move away
from its route to the water. Do it fast. Do not be between it and water.

**At night:** hippos graze away from water. Encountering one on a path at
night is possible. Same principle: identify direction to water, get out of
that line.

## Smaller but Serious Threats

### Venomous Invertebrates (spiders, scorpions)

**Prevention is everything here.**
Shake out clothing and footwear before putting on — this single habit
prevents the majority of scorpion and spider envenomations.
Check sleeping areas: scorpions like dark, dry, warm spots.
Gloves when moving rocks or logs.

**Scorpion stings:**
Most species: painful, not fatal in healthy adults.
Exceptions: bark scorpion (North America), fat-tailed scorpion (Africa/Middle East),
deathstalker (Middle East). Medical attention for all.
Signs of serious envenomation: difficulty breathing, blurred vision,
muscle spasms, drooling, numbness.

**Spider bites:**
Most species: not medically significant.
Serious species (geography-dependent): funnel-web (Australia, can be fatal
without antivenom), black widow (neurotoxic, rarely fatal), brown recluse
(necrotic). Redback (Australia), Sydney funnel-web: immediate medical care.

O'Shea's register applies here: most spider encounters are the spider
defending itself from your accidental proximity. Respect the habitat.

---

### Large Birds (cassowaries, ostriches, emus)

Rarely considered, genuinely dangerous.

**Cassowaries (Australia/New Guinea):** kick forward with a large inner
claw. A cassowary kick has killed humans. Do not approach. Do not
get between a cassowary and its young. Same rule as every other animal.

**Ostriches / emus:** kick strength comparable. Males can be territorial
in breeding season. Similar rules: make yourself large, back away, never
run. Hales would describe encountering an emu as "a bit of a situation."
