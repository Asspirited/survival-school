### MOUNTAIN / CLIMBING

**Touching the Void (1985)**
Joe Simpson and Simon Yates, Siula Grande, Peruvian Andes, 20,813ft.
Simpson broke his leg near the summit descending the West Face.
Yates lowered him on rope in darkness and blizzard. Simpson went over
a cliff edge, dangling in void. Yates held on for an hour, being pulled
off the mountain. Cut the rope to save himself.
Simpson fell into a crevasse. Survived on an ice shelf.
Spent three days crawling five miles with broken leg, no food, no water,
hallucinating. Arrived at base camp hours before Yates planned to leave.
Yates had assumed him dead. Simpson had lost 42 pounds in three days.
The rope-cutting became a mountaineering ethics debate.
Panel gold: Yates's decision. The rope. The crawl. Bony Bony Bony (the
song Simpson couldn't get out of his head and has never forgiven).

**Aron Ralston — 127 Hours (2003)**
Bluejohn Canyon, Utah. Solo, told nobody where he was going.
Boulder fell, pinned right wrist to canyon wall. 800 pounds.
Five days. Ran out of food and water. Drank own urine.
Carved name, DOB, and presumed death date into canyon wall.
Filmed goodbye messages to family.
Had a vision of himself playing with a child with one arm.
Broke his own radius and ulna using torque against the boulder.
Amputated forearm using a dull $15 multitool knife — process took an hour.
Saved arteries until last. Used CamelBak tubing as tourniquet.
Rappelled 65ft one-armed, hiked 7 miles, found by a family on holiday.
The multitool: "not a Leatherman, what you'd get if you bought a $15
flashlight and got a free multi-use tool."
Panel gold: the knife. The note. Drinking own urine when Londis
(approximately 20 miles) away. Bear's favourite story — could have
been him. Was almost literally him.

**Andes Plane Crash — Alive (1972)**
Uruguayan rugby team, Flight 571. Friday 13th October.
Crashed into Andes peak in poor visibility. 45 passengers.
12 dead in crash, more died from injuries. 8 died in later avalanche.
Remaining 16 survived 72 days in freezing conditions, no food.
Resorted to cannibalism of the dead.
Nando Parrado and Roberto Canessa trekked out — 10 days, found help.
16 survivors rescued.
Panel gold: the cannibalism decision. Packham has no framework for this.
Darwin finds it anthropologically interesting. Fox assesses it as
tactically rational. Cody notes cattails were not available.

**Hugh Glass — The Revenant (1823)**
Fur trapping expedition, Montana. Surprised a grizzly with cubs.
Extensive mauling — mutilated to the bone in places, severely broken leg.
His team left him for dead. Woke up alone, no weapons, no equipment.
Set his own leg. Wrapped himself in bearskin. Ate berries and roots.
Crawled and walked for six weeks, made a raft to float a river.
Eventually found help. Survived to confront those who abandoned him.
Panel gold: the will to survive driven by desire for revenge. Fox considers
this a legitimate operational objective. Cody approves of the methodology.

**Douglas Mawson — Antarctic Solo (1913)**
Far Eastern Party, Antarctica. Lost both companions.
One fell into a crevasse taking most of their food.
Mawson continued alone — crossing glaciers, battling blizzards.
Evidence suggests he may have eaten his dead companion to survive.
Arrived at base camp emaciated, frostbitten, weeks later.
Missed the ship home by hours. Had to winter-over another year.
Face now on the Australian $100 bill.
Disputed: whether he ate Mertz deliberately hastened Mertz's death.
Panel gold: the $100 bill. The cannibalism question. Darwin's temporal
lens fires here — expeditionary science and its costs.

### ANIMAL ENCOUNTERS

**Todd Orr — Double Grizzly Attack (2016)**
Montana hike. Encountered mother grizzly with cubs.
Used bear spray — didn't stop the charge.
Lay face-down, covered head and neck. Bear attacked, then left.
Orr ran for his vehicle. Same bear charged again further down trail.
Second attack more severe — major injuries to head, arms, shoulders.
Drove himself to hospital. Survived.
Panel gold: attacked twice by the same bear. Fox's tactical assessment:
"You failed to neutralise the threat on the first contact. That's on you."
Cody: "You should have been making noise throughout." Hales: "Yeah, nah."

**Komodo Dragon attacks**
Komodo dragons: ambush predators, venomous saliva with 50+ bacteria.
A bite doesn't kill immediately — septicaemia over days is historically
the primary mechanism. Recent research: venom glands in lower jaw.
Attacks on humans: mostly tourists who got too close.
2009: German tourist lost four fingers at Komodo National Park.
Komodo bites cause massive blood loss and infection.
Treatment: antibiotics immediately, wound debridement, antivenom.
Panel gold: Stevens finds this spiritually significant. O'Shea has
written the academic paper. Fox assesses the dragon's territorial range.

**Roy Sullivan — Lightning, 7 times (1942-1977)**
US park ranger, Virginia. Struck by lightning seven times and survived.
Known as "the Human Lightning Rod."
Lost toenail (1942), eyebrows burned off (1969), left shoulder seared
(1970), hair set on fire (1972 and 1973), ankle injured (1976), chest
and stomach burned (1977).
Panel gold: Attenborough would narrate this as evolutionary experiment.
Fox would assess the lightning as a recurring threat and recommend
changing patrol routes. Stroud would play harmonica.

### OCEAN / WATER

**Steven Callahan — 76 Days Adrift (1982)**
Solo Atlantic crossing, sloop sank 6 days out.
Survived 76 days on inflatable life raft.
Caught fish and birds. Used solar still for water.
Spearfished to supplement rations. Eaten by dorado fish for
most of the crossing (the fish following his raft, not eating him —
though he did eat them).
Found off Guadeloupe by fishermen.
Panel gold: the dorado. Stroud: "I filmed worse than that alone in Canada."

**Harrison Okene — 60 Hours Underwater (2013)**
Tugboat Jascon 4 sank off Nigeria.
Okene survived 60 hours in an air pocket inside the overturned vessel,
12 metres down.
Rescued by divers who were expecting to recover bodies.
Panel gold: Fox had assessed the escape route correctly. The air pocket
is load-bearing tactical thinking. Attenborough: "The pocket of air —
so small, so improbable — becomes the margin between one outcome and another."

**Tami Oldham Ashcraft — Hurricane (1983)**
Delivering a 44ft yacht, Tahiti to San Diego.
Category 4 hurricane Raymond. 50ft waves. Yacht capsized.
Knocked unconscious for 27 hours. Boyfriend gone, safety line broken.
Improvised mast and sail. Rationed supplies. Navigated 1,500 miles to Hawaii.
Panel gold: 27 hours unconscious. The improvised mast. Stroud's energy.

### DESERT / EXTREME ENVIRONMENT

**Mauro Prosperi — Lost in Sahara (1994)**
Marathon des Sables, Morocco. 6-day endurance race.
Sandstorm blew him 300km off course.
Survived 9 days before being found — in Algeria, not Morocco.
Drank his own urine. Ate snakes, bats, and insects.
Wrote a farewell note in his own blood when he thought death was certain.
Panel gold: the farewell note in blood. Bear: "Hydration. Critical."
Hales: "Snakes are fine. Bats too." Cody on insects: very pleased.

**Juliane Koepcke — LANSA 508 (1971)**
Lightning struck plane over Amazon. Plane broke apart at 10,000ft.
Koepcke fell still strapped to her seat. Lost consciousness.
Woke alone in Amazon rainforest. Only survivor of 92 passengers.
Survived 11 days alone, following a stream to find help.
17 years old.
Panel gold: falling 10,000ft in a seat. Surviving. Being 17.
Irwin would want to know about the wildlife she encountered.

### PART 2 — FEATURE DESIGN: "WHAT ARE THE ODDS?" / ANIMAL INCIDENT MODE

Working title: **WHAT ARE THE ODDS?**
(Alternative: **YOU'VE DONE WHAT?** / **HOW BAD IS THIS?**)

---

## Feature Concept

User describes an animal encounter or survival incident.
Panel assesses the medical/survival situation, likely outcome,
what to do RIGHT NOW, and argues about it in character.

Different from How Screwed Am I? in one key way:
**the situation has already happened.** You're not lost — you've been bitten.
You didn't disturb a bear — you've already disturbed it.
The panel is assessing immediate triage, not general survival probability.

This feeds directly into Animal Deathmatch data — same animal database,
same attributes, different application.

---

## Input Structure

**Step 1: What happened?**
Category selector:
- BITTEN / STUNG
- ATTACKED / MAULED
- STALKED / CORNERED
- TRAPPED / PINNED
- ENVIRONMENTAL (avalanche, lightning, flood, temperature)
- OTHER

**Step 2: The animal / agent**
Free text OR selector from animal database (builds over time).
Starting animals: king cobra, puff adder, black mamba, Brazilian wandering
spider, box jellyfish, great white shark, killer whale, saltwater crocodile,
grizzly bear, polar bear, black bear, komodo dragon, lion, hyena pack,
hippo, cape buffalo, cassowary, bull elephant, moose (surprisingly deadly),
Portuguese man o' war.
Comedy animals: dolphin, manatee, swan, goose, seagull (chip theft injury),
domestic cat, squirrel.

**Step 3: Circumstances**
- Location (jungle / ocean / desert / urban / arctic / moorland)
- Who's with you (alone / trained medic / clueless friend / children)
- Proximity to help (remote / 2hrs from hospital / urban / helicopter available)
- What you have (nothing / basic first aid kit / full kit / vehicle)
- Time elapsed since incident

**Step 4: Specific incident details** (freetext)
"The komodo dragon bit my ankle 20 minutes ago"
"I'm being circled by three hyenas, it's getting dark"
"The Brazilian wandering spider is currently on my chest and I haven't moved"

## Archetypal Scenarios (first build — pre-loaded)

These are based on real incident types, panel-ready, immediately testable:

**SS-INCIDENT-001: King Cobra bite, jungle, alone, 45 minutes from help**
Reference: O'Shea's published literature. Stevens's spiritual connection.
Panel disagreement: antivenom availability, tourniquet debate (don't use one
for neurotoxic venom — most people get this wrong).

**SS-INCIDENT-002: Grizzly encounter, charging, Montana trail, 3 miles from car**
Reference: Todd Orr. Panel debate: bear spray vs stand ground vs run.
Cody on making noise proactively. Fox on threat neutralisation.
Bear: has a personal anecdote. Attenborough: narrates the charge.

**SS-INCIDENT-003: Great white shark circling, surfing, 400m from shore**
Panel debate: eye contact, splash, play dead, punch the nose myth.
Stevens: "I once swam with a white pointer." Fox: assesses exit routes.

**SS-INCIDENT-004: Brazilian wandering spider on chest, in bed, 3am**
One of the most venomous spiders alive. Aggressive. Bites when disturbed.
Panel: do not move. Stevens: breathes slowly. O'Shea: has opinions.
Fox: assesses the extraction problem tactically. Hales: "Yeah, nah."

**SS-INCIDENT-005: Hyena pack, stalking, savannah, dark in 40 minutes**
3 spotted hyenas, closing in, you're alone, no vehicle.
Panel debate: fire, noise, size illusion, running (don't).
Attenborough: narrates the closing distance.

**SS-INCIDENT-006: Lightning, 7th strike (Roy Sullivan tribute)**
For comedy value. Panel cannot explain this.
Darwin: seventeen questions about evolutionary adaptation.
Fox: assesses whether lightning can be used tactically. (It cannot.)

**SS-INCIDENT-007: Komodo dragon bite, 20 minutes ago, Komodo Island**
Venomous, septic, blood not clotting. 2 hours from medical help.
O'Shea has the academic paper. Stevens considers it communion.
Practical: clean wound, compress, get to hospital immediately.

**SS-INCIDENT-008: Manatee incident (comedy)**
You have been injured by a manatee. This requires explanation.
Panel must take this entirely seriously. They do.
Attenborough's verdict on the manatee is the entire joke.

**SS-INCIDENT-009: Swan attack (comedy)**
You are being attacked by a swan. You cannot leave.
Panel's tactical assessment of a swan is extraordinary.
Fox: "That is a threat. Treat it as a threat."

**SS-INCIDENT-010: Andes situation (reference: Flight 571)**
72 days. Food is gone. There are... options.
Panel must address the question directly.
Packham leaves the room. Darwin finds it anthropologically predictable.
Cody notes the protein value. Attenborough closes.

## Panel Response Logic

**Immediate triage** (first 2 responses): Ray and Fox.
Ray: what to do RIGHT NOW, craft-based, no drama.
Fox: tactical assessment — what is the threat, is it still active, what are your exit options.

**Medical** (response 3): O'Shea or Stevens depending on animal.
O'Shea: the academic paper. References chapter numbers.
Stevens: the spiritual interpretation of what just happened.

**Comedy** (responses 4-6): Bear, Hales, Attenborough.
Bear: anecdote, abroad, fine in the end, hydration.
Hales: three words.
Attenborough: narrates your situation as nature documentary.

**Death trigger**: fires when survival probability drops below 35%,
OR when the animal's lethality stat combined with time elapsed and
treatment available crosses the threshold.
